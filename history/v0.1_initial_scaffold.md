## Self-assessment and open questions

### Tone calibration

The Core sections aim for the Alton Brown register: direct, conversational,
no jargon without introduction. The Expansion 1 sections add
worked examples with explicit dialog. Expansion 2 adds the "what this is NOT"
corrective framing. Expansion 3 is deliberately more formal.

**Open question:** Is the Core level accessible enough? The sentence
"Category theory is the study of things and arrows between things" is
deliberately blunt. Some readers may want one more sentence of context before
the formal vocabulary starts. Worth watching in reader feedback.

### The internal-dialog boxes

The dialog boxes model explicit out-loud reasoning. Example from §1.2:

> *I see the expression f : A → B. Here is what I say to myself:*
> - *"f is the name of a morphism."*
> - *"A is where f starts---its domain."*
> - *"The arrow → tells me this is a one-directional connection."*
> - *"I do not assume I can go from B back to A..."*

**Decision:** Keep the dialog boxes at this level of granularity. They model
a skill, not just content. The goal is that by Chapter 3, a reader is
producing this monologue internally without prompting.

### Right-to-left composition notation

We use $g \circ f$ (standard mathematical convention, "g after f") which
reads right-to-left. This is acknowledged in §1.3 Core: "This will feel
backwards at first. We'll practice until it feels natural."

**Open question:** Should we also introduce the diagrammatic order notation
($f;g$ or $f \cdot g$, left-to-right) in Expansion 3? Some modern CT texts
(especially those aimed at CS audiences) use left-to-right. Deferred for now;
will revisit when we write about functors, where the notation pressure is higher.

### Commutative diagrams section

The pasting lemma appears in Expansion 3 of §1.5. The natural language
translation example (English/French sentence formalization) is unusual for a
CT book but fits the "no borrowed math" constraint.

**Open question:** Is the sentence-formalization example concrete enough?
It may be too abstract for readers without linguistic intuition. Consider
adding a second example (perhaps a recipe-variant) in a revision.

### What comes next (Chapter 2 draft)

Chapter 2 will define a **category** as a package: a collection of objects, a
collection of morphisms with domains and codomains, a composition operation,
and an identity assignment---satisfying associativity and the identity laws.

It will include:
- The formal definition (Core)
- Several small concrete categories as Expansion 1 examples:
  - The "single object" category (a monoid in disguise)
  - The "discrete" category (objects only, only identity morphisms)
  - The "two-object, one-morphism" category
  - The ordering category on {small, medium, large}
- Discussion of what makes something NOT a category (Expansion 2)
- The formal axioms written out precisely (Expansion 3)

## Decisions made in this round

| Decision | Rationale |
|---|---|
| No borrowed mathematical examples in Chapter 1 | Core constraint from project brief |
