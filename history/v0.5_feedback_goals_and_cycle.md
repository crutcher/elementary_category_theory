# v0.5 — Feedback: Goal Clarification and Cycle Design

**Date:** 2026-05-11
**Covers:** Overall book goals; full cycle structure; chapter bookends;
label vocabulary; summary/restatement coexistence

---

## Feedback item 1: Explicit goal statement

The book's goal is:

> Walk the user into a **full formal understanding of category theory and
> its entire suite of theorems**, and then the popular connections to other
> branches of mathematics.

This is not a popularization. It is not dumbing the material down. It is
structuring the material as a **long, smooth, mildly entertaining ramp**:
- Incremental retraining, not front-loaded difficulty
- Cyclic redundancy by design — readers are expected to start over frequently
- Concept acquisition is the optimization target, not coverage speed

Connections to other branches of mathematics (algebra, topology, logic, etc.)
appear *after* the CT foundations are built from scratch — not as motivating
examples before the reader has the vocabulary to appreciate them.

---

## Feedback item 2: The full cycle

The learning cycle is:

```
Informal → Expanding → Formalizing → Reorganizing → Simplifying → Formally
```

Glossed:
- **Informal**: The intuition or everyday-language statement of the concept
- **Expanding**: Examples, internal dialog, multiple modalities — walking in
- **Formalizing**: Introducing precise definitions and notation
- **Reorganizing**: Restructuring the earlier examples *using* the formalism —
  showing that the formal language captures exactly what we said informally
- **Simplifying**: Annotated compression — substituting formalism for prose,
  piece by piece, watching the statement shrink
- **Formally**: The CT-idiomatic compressed form — what a category theorist
  would write and what a trained reader can now read fluently

### Design notes

- This is a **design pattern**, not a rigid template. The six labels name a
  small vocabulary; individual sections use as many steps as the concept
  warrants, reusing labels freely.
- We don't need to hit every step every time, but we should try to reuse
  labels from this vocabulary (not invent new ones ad hoc).
- The cycle should be **openly acknowledged** to the reader — they should
  know this is happening, not discover it by inference.

## Feedback item 3: Chapter bookends

When a chapter contains **multiple cycles**, it gets bookends:

- **Overview** (chapter intro): The informal/intuitive starting point for
  everything in the chapter — where the reader is before the chapter begins.
  A collection of the informal statements of the chapter's concepts.
- **Restatement** (chapter conclusion): The formal/CT-idiomatic compressed
  form of everything in the chapter — where the reader arrives after the
  full set of cycles. A collection of the formal statements.

When a chapter (or section grouping) contains **only one cycle**, the
bookends are unnecessary — the cycle itself serves as its own beginning and end.

The overview and restatement are mirror images: a reader can read the
overview, work through the chapter, arrive at the restatement, and
recognize that the restatement *is* the overview — just in a different
language they now speak.

---

## Feedback item 4: Summary box and formal restatement coexist

Keep both:
- **Summary box**: Human-language recap. Good for first-pass skimmers and
  review. Lives at the natural end of the chapter body.
- **Formal restatement**: CT-idiomatic compressed form. The conclusion of
  the chapter's cycle arc. Follows the summary box.

---

## Decisions made

| Item | Decision |
|------|----------|
| Book goal | Full formal CT + theorems + math connections; long smooth ramp |
| Cycle | Informal → Expanding → Formalizing → Reorganizing → Simplifying → Formally |
| Label vocabulary | Small, reused; not dogmatically applied every time |
| Openness | The cycle is acknowledged to the reader explicitly |
| Bookends | Overview (intro) + Restatement (conclusion) when chapter has multiple cycles |
| Single-cycle material | No bookends needed |
| Summary + restatement | Both kept; summary = human recap, restatement = formal compression |
